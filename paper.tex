\begin{abstract}
% ============================================================
We present FiZK (Federated Interpretability with Zero Knowledge), a
hierarchical federated learning architecture that unifies Merkle-tree
commitment, zero-knowledge gradient validation, Byzantine-robust
aggregation, and IVC-based proof folding into a single verifiable
security pipeline. The system operates across four phases.
In Phase~1, every edge client computes its gradient and generates a
zero-knowledge proof (via the ProtoGalaxy IVC scheme on BN254/Grumpkin
curves) attesting that the gradient sum is correctly computed and that
$\|\nabla w_i\|_2 \leq B$, where $B$ is derived from
$\mathrm{median} \pm k \cdot \mathrm{MAD}$ of historical norms.
In Phase~2, the Galaxy Aggregator verifies Merkle commitments and
ZK proofs, pruning any client whose proof fails before weights enter
aggregation. In Phase~3, Byzantine-robust selection via Multi-Krum
filters the surviving gradients, and galaxy-level IVC folding
compresses per-client proofs into a single galaxy proof
$\pi_g^{\mathsf{fold}}$.
In Phase~4, the Global Server verifies galaxy Merkle roots against
the global tree and applies the secure model update.
Evaluation on MNIST with 10 clients under adaptive, label-flip, and
Gaussian noise attacks demonstrates that FiZK-Full (ZK+Merkle)
maintains 92.06\% accuracy at 50\% Byzantine fraction---where
Multi-Krum alone drops to 90.85\%---at a cost of approximately
$5.9\times$ overhead due to CPU-bound proof generation.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% ============================================================

Federated Learning (FL) enables collaborative model training without
centralizing raw data \cite{li2020federated,kairouz2021advances}.
By exchanging only model updates, FL has found application in
privacy-sensitive domains such as healthcare, finance, and mobile
intelligence. Yet the distributed nature of FL creates attack surfaces
that existing defenses address only partially and in isolation.

\subsection{Motivation}

Five fundamental challenges undermine FL security.
\emph{Byzantine attacks} allow malicious clients to inject poisoned
gradients that degrade accuracy or embed hidden backdoors
\cite{blanchard2017machine,fang2020local}.
The \emph{aggregator trust problem} means that even a formally correct
aggregation algorithm can be subverted by a compromised aggregator that
modifies or selectively drops updates without detection.
The \emph{attribution problem} prevents post-hoc forensic analysis:
without cryptographic evidence it is impossible to prove which gradient
came from which client.
An \emph{accountability gap} leaves no mechanism for compliance audit
or dispute resolution.
Finally, \emph{scalability limitations} of existing robust aggregators, notably the $O(n^2)$ complexity of Multi-Krum constrain
deployment to small federations.

Existing solutions address these challenges in isolation. Robust
aggregation (Krum \cite{blanchard2017machine}, Trimmed Mean
\cite{yin2018byzantine}) provides Byzantine resilience but cannot
prove that the aggregation algorithm was applied faithfully. Secure
aggregation \cite{bonawitz2017practical} hides individual updates but
is blind to Byzantine behavior.
FLTrust \cite{cao2021fltrust}
achieves strong accuracy but requires a clean labeled dataset at the
server, conflicting with data-minimization requirements. 

\subsection{Key Insight: ZK-First, Fold-Then-Verify}

FiZK closes this gap with two cryptographic primitives. First,
each client generates a zero-knowledge proof attesting that
its gradient sum is correctly computed and that
$\|\nabla w_i\|_2 \leq B$ (where $B$ is derived from the
server-side norm distribution via $\mathrm{median} \pm k \cdot \mathrm{MAD}$).
This ZK-Firewall gate enforces a hard safety
envelope on every gradient before any weight enters aggregation,
eliminating high-magnitude attacks at the proof-validation stage.
Second, at the galaxy level, the aggregator applies
IVC-based folding via the ProtoGalaxy scheme
\cite{kothapalli2023protogalaxy} (implemented through the Sonobe
library \cite{sonobe2024} on BN254/Grumpkin curves) to compress
per-client proofs into a single galaxy proof $\pi_g^{\mathsf{fold}}$.

\subsection{Contributions}

This paper makes five contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Four-phase verifiable FL pipeline}: A formally specified
  commit-validate-fold protocol (Phase~1: client commitment and ZKP
  generation; Phase~2: Merkle and ZK verification; Phase~3: Multi-Krum
  selection and galaxy-level IVC folding; Phase~4: global Merkle
  verification and model update).

\item \textbf{ZK-Firewall}: A zero-knowledge gate proving gradient sum
  correctness and norm boundedness ($\|\nabla w_i\|_2 \leq B$) via a
  2-constraint arithmetic circuit, enforced before any gradient enters
  aggregation.

\item \textbf{Galaxy-Level IVC Folding}: Application of the ProtoGalaxy
  IVC folding scheme (via Sonobe on BN254/Grumpkin) to compress
  per-client proofs within each galaxy into a single folded proof
  $\pi_g^{\mathsf{fold}}$.

\item \textbf{Merkle Integrity Lock}: A two-level hierarchical Merkle
  tree providing $O(\log n)$ gradient commitment verification and an
  immutable forensic audit trail linking every client submission to a
  published root.

\item \textbf{Empirical evaluation}: Comparison across adaptive,
  label-flip, and Gaussian noise attacks on MNIST at 10-client scale,
  demonstrating that ZK-based filtering extends effective Byzantine
  tolerance beyond Multi-Krum's theoretical bound of $f < n/2 - 2$.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Threat Model and Assumptions}
\label{sec:threat}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Adversary Capabilities}

We consider a strong adaptive adversary. At the \emph{client level}, up
to $\alpha = 30\%$ of clients are Byzantine, may collude, and can submit
arbitrary gradients (label-flipping, backdoor injection, model
poisoning, Gaussian noise). The adversary observes historical
aggregations to adapt its strategy and may attempt Sybil attacks within
the identity-registration constraint. At the \emph{aggregator level},
the galaxy aggregator may modify gradients after reception, selectively
exclude honest clients, or falsely attribute malicious submissions.
At the \emph{network level}, a man-in-the-middle may intercept and
modify gradients in transit or replay previously valid submissions.

\subsection{Security Assumptions}

We assume: (i)~clients trust their own local computations;
(ii)~a strict majority of clients ($>70\%$) are honest;
(iii)~SHA-256 and the underlying ZK argument system are computationally
secure; (iv)~messages are delivered within bounded time;
(v)~each client holds a unique public/private key pair.

\subsection{Security Goals}

Six security properties are targeted. \emph{Integrity}: gradients are
not tampered with during transmission or aggregation.
\emph{Norm bounding}: no gradient with $\|\nabla w_i\|_2 \geq T$ can
enter the aggregation pipeline.
\emph{Byzantine resilience}: final model retains $>95\%$ of clean
accuracy despite up to 30\% malicious clients.
\emph{Attribution}: malicious clients are identified with $>90\%$
true-positive rate and $<5\%$ false-positive rate.
\emph{Accountability}: every gradient submission is backed by a
cryptographic proof enabling post-hoc forensic analysis.
\emph{Availability}: system operates with $<20\%$ overhead over
vanilla FL.

\section{FiZK Architecture}
\label{sec:arch}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Four-Phase Protocol Overview}

FiZK structures every training round into four distinct, verifiable phases to ensure strict isolation between commitment, validation, aggregation, and global update, as illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:arch}.

\begin{figure*}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{architecture.jpg}
    \caption{The FiZK Pipeline. Phase~1: clients generate gradients, Merkle commitments, and ZK proofs (gradient sum correctness + norm bound). Phase~2: galaxy aggregator verifies Merkle integrity and ZK proofs. Phase~3: Multi-Krum selection and galaxy-level IVC folding via ProtoGalaxy. Phase~4: global Merkle verification and model update.}
    \label{fig:arch}
\end{figure*}

\paragraph{Phase 1: Client Commitment \& ZKP Generation}
Each client $i$ trains on private dataset $D_i$ to produce gradient $\nabla w_i^{(r)}$. The client then generates:
\begin{itemize}
    \item A cryptographic commitment $h_i = H(\nabla w_i^{(r)} \|\, r \|\, \mathsf{nonce})$;
    \item A zero-knowledge proof $\pi_i^{zk}$ attesting that the gradient sum is correctly computed and that $\|\nabla w_i\|_2 \leq B$, where $B$ is the server-published norm bound derived from $\mathrm{median} \pm k \cdot \mathrm{MAD}$ of historical gradient norms.
\end{itemize}
The client sends $(h_i, \pi_i^{zk})$ to the Galaxy Aggregator. The Galaxy Aggregator constructs a Merkle tree from all $\{h_i\}$ and publishes the galaxy root $R_g^{(r)}$, locking the commitments.

\paragraph{Phase 2: Verification \& ZK-Firewall}
Clients reveal their gradient $\nabla w_i^{(r)}$. The Galaxy Aggregator performs two-stage validation:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Merkle Integrity:} Verify $h_i$ matches the locked Merkle root $R_g^{(r)}$.
    \item \textbf{ZK-Firewall:} Verify $\pi_i^{zk}$ against server-side norm bounds. Invalid proofs trigger immediate rejection.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Phase 3: Byzantine-Robust Aggregation \& IVC Folding}
Gradients surviving Phase~2 proceed through Multi-Krum selection and statistical anomaly detection. The aggregator simultaneously applies ProtoGalaxy IVC folding (via the Sonobe library on BN254/Grumpkin curves) to compress per-client ZK proofs into a single galaxy proof:
\begin{equation}
\pi_g^{\mathsf{fold}} \leftarrow \mathsf{IVC.Fold}(\dots \mathsf{IVC.Fold}(\pi_1^{zk}, \pi_2^{zk}), \dots)
\end{equation}
The aggregator transmits $(\bar{w}_g, \pi_g^{\mathsf{fold}}, R_g)$ to the Global Server.

\paragraph{Phase 4: Global Verification \& Model Update}
The Global Server collects galaxy submissions $\{\bar{w}_g, \pi_g^{\mathsf{fold}}, R_g\}_{g=1}^G$. It constructs a global Merkle tree from the galaxy roots and verifies each $R_g$ against the global root $R$. Upon successful verification, the server applies the aggregated update to produce $w^{(r+1)}$. The global Merkle root $R$ serves as a cryptographic anchor for post-hoc audit.

\textbf{Note:} ZK proof verification is performed at the \emph{galaxy level} (Phase~2), not at the global server. The global server trusts the galaxy aggregators to have correctly filtered clients via ZKP, and performs only Merkle-root consistency checks.

\subsection{Hierarchical Merkle Tree}

Two Merkle trees operate concurrently to ensure binding integrity. The
\emph{galaxy tree} constructs leaves from a hash of the
gradient and metadata (round number, nonce):
\begin{equation}
h_i = H(\nabla w_i \|\, r \|\, \mathsf{nonce}_i)
\end{equation}
This yields the galaxy root $R_g$. The \emph{global tree} is
constructed from the galaxy roots $\{R_1, \ldots, R_G\}$, yielding the
global root $R$. Crucially, galaxy roots are published \emph{before}
gradients are revealed, preventing adaptive attacks.

\textbf{Claim 3.1 (Binding Commitment):} Under the collision-resistance
of SHA-256, once $R_g$ is published, no adversary can produce a
gradient $\nabla w' \neq \nabla w_i$ that yields
the same leaf hash $h_i$, ensuring gradient integrity.

\section{ZK-Firewall and ProtoGalaxy}
\label{sec:zkp}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Client-Side ZKP: Gradient Sum \& Norm Integrity}

Each client generates a zero-knowledge proof demonstrating two properties of its gradient without revealing the gradient itself. The prover demonstrates knowledge of a witness $w = (\nabla w_i, \mathrm{aux})$ satisfying the relation $\mathcal{R}_{\mathrm{zk}}$:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{R}_{\mathrm{zk}} = \left\{
(\nabla w_i, x_{\mathrm{pub}}) :
\begin{aligned}
&1.\; \sum_j (\nabla w_i)_j = S_i \text{ (Sum correctness)} \\
&2.\; \|\nabla w_i\|_2 \leq B
\end{aligned}
\right\}
\label{eq:zk_relation}
\end{equation}
where $B$ is determined from the server-side norm distribution via $\mathrm{median} \pm k \cdot \mathrm{MAD}$ (Median Absolute Deviation) of historical gradient norms. The proof is generated using ProtoGalaxy IVC on BN254/Grumpkin curves via the Sonobe library \cite{sonobe2024}.

\textbf{Important distinction:} Unlike some zkML approaches, the current ZK circuit does \emph{not} prove that the gradient was derived from a specific dataset or that SGD was executed correctly. It provides verifiable norm enforcement and sum-check integrity. Dataset commitment and proof-of-training are discussed as future extensions in Section~\ref{sec:future}.

\subsection{ZK-Firewall Gate}

Upon receiving $(\nabla w_i, \pi_i^{zk})$, the Galaxy Aggregator executes a strict ZK-Firewall:
\begin{equation}
\mathsf{Valid}_i =
\begin{cases}
1 & \text{if } \mathsf{Verify}(\pi_i^{zk}, x_{\mathrm{pub}}) = \top \\
0 & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\label{eq:gate}
\end{equation}
Clients with $\mathsf{Valid}_i = 0$ are immediately pruned and their reputation score is penalized. This creates a \emph{hard cryptographic gate} that filters gradients violating the norm bound before they reach the statistical defense layers.

\textbf{Theorem 4.1 (Soundness of Norm Enforcement).}
Under the soundness of the ZK scheme, any accepted client $i \in \mathcal{V}$ must satisfy $\|\nabla w_i\|_2 \leq B$. Consequently, for the aggregated update $\bar{w}_g = \frac{1}{m} \sum_{i \in \mathcal{S}} \nabla w_i$, we have $\|\bar{w}_g\|_2 \leq B$, ensuring the global model update remains bounded.

\subsection{Galaxy-Level IVC Folding}

After Multi-Krum selection produces the set $\mathcal{S}$ of $m$ accepted clients, the aggregator holds $m$ valid ZK proofs $\{\pi_i^{zk}\}_{i \in \mathcal{S}}$. Transmitting all $m$ proofs would incur linear verification cost. To solve this, we employ Incremental Verifiable Computation (IVC) based on the ProtoGalaxy folding scheme \cite{bunz2023protogalaxy}.

\paragraph{Sequential IVC Accumulation}
Let $\mathcal{F}_{\mathsf{IVC}}$ be the folding function. Unlike tree-based aggregation, IVC folds proofs sequentially into a single running accumulator. Starting with an initial accumulator $\mathrm{acc}_1 = \pi_1^{zk}$, the Galaxy Aggregator iterates:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{acc}_{k} = \mathcal{F}_{\mathsf{IVC}}(\mathrm{acc}_{k-1}, \pi_k^{zk}), \quad k=2,\dots,m.
\label{eq:ivc_fold}
\end{equation}
Each step compresses the verification of the new proof $\pi_k^{zk}$ into the existing state. The final accumulator $\pi_g^{\mathsf{fold}} = \mathrm{acc}_m$ is a single succinct proof that attests to the validity of the entire sequence of $m$ client computations.

\paragraph{Constant-Time Output}
Crucially, the size of $\pi_g^{\mathsf{fold}}$ is independent of $m$. This reduces the verification complexity at the next level from $O(m)$ to $O(1)$, matching the cost of verifying a single ZK proof.

\subsection{Transmitted Artifacts}
The Galaxy Aggregator transmits to the Global Server:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The galaxy IVC proof $\pi_g^{\mathsf{fold}}$;
    \item The aggregated weight vector $\bar{w}_g$;
    \item The galaxy Merkle root $R_g$ for audit linkage.
\end{itemize}
The Global Server verifies Merkle-root consistency (that each $R_g$ anchors correctly into the global tree $R$) but does not re-verify individual ZK proofs, relying on the galaxy aggregator's Phase~2 filtering.

\subsection{Operational Modes}

We evaluate the system in two configurations corresponding to the architecture's ablation study:

\paragraph{FiZK-Lite (Merkle-only)}
This baseline mode enables SHA-256 Merkle commitments and Multi-Krum aggregation but disables the ZK pipeline (ZK-Firewall and IVC Folding). It represents a standard robust FL setup with commit-reveal integrity but without zero-knowledge verification.

\paragraph{FiZK-Full (ZK-Merkle)}
The complete architecture is active: client-side ZKP generation via ProtoGalaxy IVC, ZK-Firewall pruning at the galaxy level, and galaxy-level IVC folding.

\section{Multi-Layer Defense Stack}
\label{sec:defense}

FiZK implements a defense-in-depth framework consisting of four distinct layers. Layer~1 enforces hard cryptographic validity, while Layers~2--4 provide statistical and reputation-based filtering on the validated gradient set.

\subsection{Layer 1: Cryptographic Integrity (Merkle + ZKP)}
\label{subsec:layer1}

This layer acts as a hard gate, ensuring that no invalid or fabricated computation enters the aggregation pipeline. It executes a **Two-Stage Verification** protocol:

\paragraph{Stage 1: Merkle Integrity}
The galaxy Merkle tree ensures binding commitments. Any modification to a gradient $\nabla w_i$ after commitment $h_i$ results in a mismatch with the published root $R_g$. This strictly prevents man-in-the-middle attacks, aggregator tampering, and replay attacks (via metadata nonces).

\paragraph{Stage 2: ZKP Norm Integrity}
FiZK requires a valid ZK proof $\pi_i^{zk}$ for every submission. The ZK-Firewall verifies $\pi_i^{zk}$ against server-side norm bounds. This proves that $\|\nabla w_i\|_2 \leq B$ and that the gradient sum is correctly computed.

\textbf{Security Guarantee:} Under the soundness of the ZK system and collision resistance of SHA-256, it is computationally infeasible for an adversary to submit a gradient exceeding the norm bound or tamper with a valid commitment without detection.

\subsection{Layer 2: Statistical Anomaly Detection}

Gradients passing Layer~1 are technically valid but may be statistically anomalous (e.g., honest training on poisoned data). We apply four metrics:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{M1 (Norm Analysis):} Flag if $\|\nabla w_i\|_2$ deviates from the median by more than $k \cdot \mathrm{MAD}$, where MAD is the Median Absolute Deviation of norms in the galaxy.
    \item \textbf{M2 (Direction Similarity):} Flag if cosine similarity $\cos(\nabla w_i, \bar{g}) < 0.5$, where $\bar{g}$ is the historical momentum vector.
    \item \textbf{M3 (Coordinate-wise MAD):} Flag if parameters deviate $> k \cdot \text{MAD}$ from the coordinate-wise median.
    \item \textbf{M4 (Distribution Shift):} Flag if the KL-divergence $D_{\text{KL}}(\nabla w_i \| \hat{P})$ exceeds the dynamic threshold.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Decision Logic:} A gradient is flagged if it fails $\geq 2$ metrics.

\subsection{Layer 3: Byzantine-Robust Aggregation}

To neutralize Byzantine gradients that survive statistical filtering, we employ \textbf{Multi-Krum}. Given $n'$ accepted gradients and a Byzantine budget $f$:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Compute pairwise Euclidean distances $d_{ij} = \|\nabla w_i - \nabla w_j\|_2$.
    \item Assign score $s_i = \sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}_i} d_{ij}$, where $\mathcal{N}_i$ is the set of $k = n' - f - 2$ nearest neighbors.
    \item Select the set $\mathcal{S}$ of $m = n' - f$ gradients with the lowest scores.
    \item Compute the aggregate $\bar{w}_g = \frac{1}{m} \sum_{i \in \mathcal{S}} \nabla w_i$.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Layer 4: Reputation-Based Adaptive Trust}

We implement an exponential weighted moving average (EWMA) reputation system that prioritizes cryptographic honesty.

\textbf{Behavior Scoring:}
The behavior score $B_i(t)$ for round $t$ is calculated as:
\begin{equation}
B_i(t) = 
\begin{cases} 
0 & \text{if } I_{\text{zkp}} = 0 \quad \text{(\textbf{Hard Gate})} \\
\sum_{k} w_k I_k & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\label{eq:behavior}
\end{equation}
The weights are: ZKP Pass ($w_1 = 0.35$), Merkle Pass ($w_2 = 0.15$), Statistical Pass ($w_3 = 0.25$), Krum Selection ($w_4 = 0.25$).

\textbf{Reputation Update:}
$\mathcal{R}_i(t+1) = (1-\lambda)\mathcal{R}_i(t) + \lambda B_i(t)$ with $\lambda=0.1$.
A ZKP failure ($I_{\text{zkp}}=0$) triggers an immediate score drop, as it constitutes cryptographic evidence of norm violation. Clients with $\mathcal{R}_i < 0.2$ are quarantined.

\textbf{Note on Galaxy-Level Defense:} The architecture supports galaxy-level anomaly detection as a future extension (see Section~\ref{sec:future}). In the current implementation, the global server performs Merkle-root verification of galaxy submissions but does not quarantine entire galaxies based on reputation.

\section{Experimental Evaluation}
\label{sec:eval}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Implementation}

FiZK is implemented in \textbf{Python} with \textbf{PyTorch} for
local model training. Merkle tree construction uses \textbf{SHA-256}
via Python's standard library. Zero-knowledge proof generation and
verification are handled by a \textbf{Rust} bridge wrapping the
\textbf{Sonobe IVC library} \cite{sonobe2024}, which implements
ProtoGalaxy folding on \textbf{BN254/Grumpkin} cycle curves; the
bridge is compiled with \textbf{Maturin}. Byzantine-robust aggregation
employs \textbf{Multi-Krum}, coordinate-wise median, and trimmed mean.
Statistical anomaly detection uses norm analysis, cosine similarity,
coordinate-wise MAD, and KL-divergence. Client reputation tracking
uses an \textbf{EWMA} (Exponentially Weighted Moving Average) scheme.
Wall-clock timing uses \texttt{time.perf\_counter} throughout.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

\emph{Dataset}: MNIST (60K train, 10K test) under IID partitioning.

\emph{Model}: Linear softmax classifier.

\emph{Hyperparameters}: 10 training rounds; local epochs $= 1$;
batch size $= 64$; SGD with learning rate $0.01$; Byzantine fraction
$\alpha \in \{0.20, 0.30, 0.40, 0.50\}$; ZK norm bound $B$ derived
from $\mathrm{median} \pm k \cdot \mathrm{MAD}$ of historical norms;
aggregation via trimmed mean with trim ratio $0.1$.

\emph{Scale}: 10 clients organized into 2 galaxies (round-robin
client assignment by $\text{client\_id} \bmod G$). We acknowledge
this is a proof-of-concept scale; larger-scale experiments are
planned as future work (Section~\ref{sec:future}).

\emph{Baselines}: Vanilla FedAvg (no defense), Multi-Krum (standalone),
FiZK-Lite (Merkle~+~Multi-Krum, no ZKP), and
FiZK-Full (Merkle~+~ZKP~+~Multi-Krum~+~IVC folding).

\emph{Trials}: Single trial per configuration (seed $= 42$).

\subsection{Attack Scenarios}

Six attacks are evaluated:
(1)~adaptive attack (adversary observes historical aggregations and
calibers magnitude/direction to evade detection);
(2)~label flipping ($0 \leftrightarrow 9$);
(3)~Gaussian noise ($\nabla w_i +
\mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2 I)$);
(4)~targeted label flip (source class only);
(5)~model poisoning (scaled gradient to force
$\|\nabla w_i\|_2 \gg B$);
(6)~backdoor (pixel-pattern trigger causing targeted
misclassification).

\subsection{Limitations of Current Evaluation}

We acknowledge several limitations. (1)~All experiments use a single
trial (seed~=~42); multi-trial statistical significance testing is
planned. (2)~The 10-client, 2-galaxy scale is a proof-of-concept;
larger federations may exhibit different dynamics.
(3)~Only MNIST with IID partitioning is evaluated; non-IID settings
(where FiZK-Lite showed better performance in preliminary tests due to
more aggressive flagging) and complex datasets (CIFAR-10) remain as
future work. (4)~FLTrust was not included in the benchmark comparison.
(5)~The reputation system was not exercised in 10-round experiments
(no clients reached the quarantine threshold).

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Byzantine-Robust Federated Learning}

Blanchard et al.\ \cite{blanchard2017machine} introduced Krum and proved
Byzantine resilience for $f < n/2 - 1$. Yin et al.\
\cite{yin2018byzantine} proposed coordinate-wise median and trimmed mean
as lighter-weight alternatives. FLAME \cite{nguyen2022flame} uses noise
injection and clustering to suppress backdoors. FLTrust
\cite{cao2021fltrust} bootstraps trust from a server-held root dataset.
None of these systems provides pre-revelation gradient norm bounding or
compact folded audit proofs.

\subsection{Verifiable and Blockchain-Based FL}

Blockchain-based FL \cite{kim2020blockchained} provides global
verifiability via on-chain consensus but at latency incompatible with
synchronous training. Recent zkFL proposals apply SNARKs to prove
correct aggregation computation rather than per-client gradient
properties, and do not exploit IVC folding to reduce verification
cost. FiZK is, to our knowledge, the first system to apply the
ProtoGalaxy IVC scheme \cite{kothapalli2023protogalaxy} (via the
Sonobe library \cite{sonobe2024}) to per-client gradient proofs in
a federated learning context.

\subsection{Zero-Knowledge Proofs in Machine Learning}

The intersection of ZKPs and ML (zkML) has focused primarily on proving
correct inference of a fixed, published model. Client-side ZK proofs of
gradient properties during training represent a complementary and
largely unexplored direction. FiZK's ZK circuit is a lightweight
2-constraint system (gradient sum correctness + norm bound), distinct
from full zkML circuits that prove entire model inference. The
underlying proof system is ProtoGalaxy IVC
\cite{kothapalli2023protogalaxy}, implemented via the Sonobe library
\cite{sonobe2024} on BN254/Grumpkin cycle curves.

\subsection{Secure Aggregation}

Bonawitz et al.\ \cite{bonawitz2017practical} demonstrated practical
secure aggregation using secret sharing. While orthogonal to Byzantine
resilience, this work addresses the privacy of individual gradient
contributions. Combining FiZK's ZK-Firewall with homomorphic or
secret-share-based aggregation is a natural extension that would provide
simultaneous privacy and integrity guarantees.


% ============================================================
\section{Limitations and Future Work}
\label{sec:conc}
\label{sec:future}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Limitations}

Client-side ZK proof generation scales as $O(d)$ arithmetic operations
and currently adds $\sim$5.9$\times$ overhead, dominated by CPU-bound
proof computation ($\sim$9.7s prove + $\sim$9.7s verify per round).
This may be prohibitive for large models on resource-constrained
devices. Multi-Krum's $O(n^2 d)$ per-galaxy complexity limits galaxy
size in practice. The reputation mechanism was not exercised in our
10-round evaluations (no clients reached the quarantine threshold).
Galaxy assignment currently relies on round-robin partitioning;
optimal dynamic assignment remains an open problem. Merkle
verification provides no gradient privacy, requiring separate secure
aggregation for confidentiality. All current evaluations are at
proof-of-concept scale (10 clients, 2 galaxies, MNIST only).

\subsection{Future Work}

We identify five research directions. First, \emph{GPU-accelerated
ZK proof generation}: libraries such as Icicle or cuZK could reduce
the $\sim$10s prove time by 10--100$\times$, making the overhead
practically negligible. Second, \emph{dataset commitment and
proof-of-training}: extending the ZK circuit to prove
$\nabla w_i = \mathsf{SGD}(w^{(r-1)}, D_i)$ on committed data
$H(D_i) = C_i$ would close the remaining integrity gap. Third,
\emph{privacy integration}: combining ZK-Firewall commitments with
homomorphic encryption or differential privacy would provide
simultaneous norm bounding and gradient confidentiality. Fourth,
\emph{large-scale evaluation}: experiments with 100+ clients, multiple
galaxies, non-IID partitioning, CIFAR-10/ImageNet, and multi-trial
statistical significance. Fifth, \emph{global ProtoGalaxy folding}:
folding galaxy-level proofs $\{\pi_g^{\mathsf{fold}}\}_{g=1}^G$ into
a single global proof $\Pi$ would enable $O(1)$ global verification.


% ============================================================
\section{Conclusion}
% ============================================================

We presented FiZK, a hierarchical federated learning architecture
that integrates zero-knowledge gradient validation, Merkle-tree
commitment, Byzantine-robust aggregation, and IVC-based proof folding
into a four-phase verifiable protocol. The ZK-Firewall enforces a
hard norm envelope ($\|\nabla w_i\|_2 \leq B$) and gradient sum
correctness before any weight enters aggregation. Galaxy-level
ProtoGalaxy IVC folding compresses per-client proofs into a single
constant-size accumulator $\pi_g^{\mathsf{fold}}$ per galaxy.
Evaluation on MNIST at 10-client scale demonstrates that FiZK-Full
maintains accuracy (92.06\%) at 50\% Byzantine fraction where
Multi-Krum alone degrades (90.85\%), at a cost of $\sim$5.9$\times$
overhead dominated by CPU-bound proof operations.
FiZK provides integrity verification, norm bounding, Byzantine
resilience, and a cryptographic audit trail simultaneously,
making it suited for trustworthy FL deployment in
compliance-sensitive domains.

\section{Full Round Protocol}
\label{app:algo}
% ============================================================

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{FiZK: One Training Round $r$}
\label{alg:round}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE global model $w^{(r)}$, norm bound $B$, Byzantine bound $f$
\ENSURE updated model $w^{(r+1)}$, galaxy proofs $\{\pi_g^{\mathsf{fold}}\}$

\STATE \textbf{// Phase 1: Client Commitment \& ZKP Generation}
\FOR{each client $i$}
  \STATE $\nabla w_i \leftarrow \mathsf{LocalTrain}(w^{(r)}, D_i)$
  \STATE $h_i \leftarrow H(\nabla w_i \;\|\; r \;\|\; \mathrm{nonce})$
  \STATE $\pi_i^{zk} \leftarrow \mathsf{ZKProve}(\nabla w_i, B)$
        \quad [proves sum correctness + $\|\nabla w_i\|_2 \leq B$]
  \STATE send $(h_i,\; \pi_i^{zk})$ to Galaxy Aggregator
\ENDFOR

\STATE \textbf{// Phase 2: Galaxy Verification \& ZK-Firewall}
\STATE $R_g \leftarrow \mathsf{MerkleRoot}(\{h_i\})$; publish $R_g$
\STATE $\mathcal{V} \leftarrow \emptyset$
\FOR{each client $i$}
  \STATE request $\nabla w_i$ from client $i$
  \IF{$\mathsf{MerkleVerify}(h_i, R_g) = \top$
       \AND $\mathsf{ZKVerify}(\pi_i^{zk}, B) = \top$}
    \STATE $\mathcal{V} \leftarrow \mathcal{V} \cup \{i\}$
  \ELSE
    \STATE \textbf{prune} client $i$; penalize reputation
  \ENDIF
\ENDFOR

\STATE \textbf{// Phase 3: Multi-Krum Selection \& IVC Folding}
\STATE $\mathcal{S},\;\bar{w}_g \leftarrow \mathsf{MultiKrum}(
  \{\nabla w_i\}_{i \in \mathcal{V}},\; f)$
\STATE $\pi_g^{\mathsf{fold}} \leftarrow \mathsf{IVC.Fold}(
  \{\pi_i^{zk}\}_{i \in \mathcal{S}})$
\STATE send $(\bar{w}_g,\; \pi_g^{\mathsf{fold}},\; R_g)$ to Global Server

\STATE \textbf{// Phase 4: Global Merkle Verification \& Update}
\STATE $R \leftarrow \mathsf{GlobalMerkleRoot}(\{R_g\}_{g=1}^G)$
\FOR{each galaxy $g$}
  \IF{$\mathsf{VerifyGalaxyRoot}(R_g, R) \neq \top$}
    \STATE reject galaxy $g$
  \ENDIF
\ENDFOR
\STATE $w^{(r+1)} \leftarrow w^{(r)} + \eta\,\sum_g \bar{w}_g / G$
\STATE broadcast $w^{(r+1)}$; log $R$
\RETURN $w^{(r+1)}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}
