% ABSTRACT
% ============================================================
\begin{abstract}

Federated learning makes it possible to conduct collaborative learning without sharing original data; however, it also makes it vulnerable to various attacks, especially those from Byzantine clients, because of its distributed learning paradigm. Byzantine attacks pose a critical threat to the federated learning systems, as they may compromise the integrity and performance of the model through malicious gradients and poisoning attacks. While robust aggregation makes federated learning systems more resistant to Byzantine attacks, it does not guarantee the correctness of client updates. In this paper, we propose a novel hierarchical federated learning system called FiZK, which leverages unique features of zero-knowledge gradient validation, commitment, and robust aggregation in ensuring verifiable integrity in the federated learning paradigm. FiZK also overcomes existing defenses in handling Byzantine attacks because it provides a holistic solution to these attacks in federated learning systems. FiZK includes a ZK gradient validation layer, where gradient norms are validated before aggregation; it also leverages a commitment mechanism using a Merkle tree and proof folding to ensure efficiency in Byzantine attack handling. We demonstrate FiZK’s performance in different attack scenarios and show its robustness in ensuring accurate model performance even in the presence of high Byzantine fractions.

\end{abstract}


\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} federated learning, zero-knowledge proofs, Merkle trees, IVC folding, ProtoGalaxy.

\section{Introduction}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Motivation}

Federated Learning (FL) is a framework for collaborative model training without the exposure of sensitive raw data \cite{li2020federated,kairouz2021advances}. This is the major reason why FL is being adopted by privacy-oriented industries like the health sector, finance sector, and mobile computing. However, the process of training the model is vulnerable to security attacks, where a malicious client may send altered gradients that may lower the accuracy of the global model. 

The attacks are of different types, and they can be interconnected. Byzantine clients may send poisoned gradients, which may lower the accuracy of the global model or may plant backdoors in the global model \cite{blanchard2017machine,fang2020local}. A compromised aggregator may drop the gradients, which is known as the \emph{aggregator trust problem}. In FL, the gradients are not cryptographically tied to the clients, meaning that it is essentially impossible to identify the source of a malicious gradient. Moreover, the high computation overhead of the Multi-Krum method, which is $O(n^2)$, is a major barrier for the widespread adoption of this method. Most of the existing works address only a part of the attacks. Most of the robust aggregation methods like Krum \cite{blanchard2017machine} and Trimmed Mean \cite{yin2018byzantine} are based on the assumption that only a limited fraction of clients will submit malicious or poisoned gradients. However, there is no guarantee that the client is sending the gradients honestly. On the other hand, the secure aggregation method \cite{bonawitz2017practical} is based on the secret sharing method, which ensures the privacy of the client’s gradients. However, the method is vulnerable to Byzantine attacks. FLTrust \cite{cao2021fltrust} is based on the assumption that the server maintains a clean root dataset, which is against the data minimization principle.

\subsection{Key Insight: ZK-First, Fold-Then-Verify}

% We present FiZK, which addresses these shortcomings with two complementary cryptographic mechanisms. On every client, a zero-knowledge proof attests that the gradient sum is correct and that $\|\nabla w_i\|_2 \leq B$ (with $B$ derived from the server-side norm distribution using $\mathrm{median} \pm k \cdot \mathrm{MAD}$). This ZK-Firewall places a hard safety envelope around each gradient \emph{before} it reaches aggregation, so high-magnitude attacks are blocked at the proof-validation stage instead of being detected only after damage is done. At the galaxy level, the aggregator folds each client's proof into one compact proof $\pi_g^{\mathsf{fold}}$ using IVC-based folding through the ProtoGalaxy scheme \cite{kothapalli2023protogalaxy}, as implemented in the Sonobe library \cite{sonobe2024} on BN254/Grumpkin curves.

\textbf{FiZK} is a federated learning framework that leverages the combination of two cryptographic techniques. First, at the client side, each client provides a zero-knowledge proof that verifies that the sum of the gradients is correctly calculated and that the gradient norm is within the bound $|\nabla w_i|_2 \leq B$, with $B$ calculated based on the norm distribution using $\mathrm{median} \pm k.\mathrm{MAD}$. This method is called \emph{ZK-Firewall}. It provides a strict bound on gradients before the aggregation process.

To improve scalability, the clients within the federated learning framework are divided into clusters or groups called galaxies. Each galaxy contains a set of clients whose gradients are processed as a group by an aggregator. This approach not only eases the aggregation process but also optimizes resource allocation to improve the scalability on client side within the federated learning framework.

At the galaxy level, the aggregator uses the IVC-based folding to produce a proof $\pi_g^{\mathsf{fold}}$ based on the client-level proofs using the ProtoGalaxy \cite{kothapalli2023protogalaxy}, which is implemented using the Sonobe library \cite{sonobe2024} with BN254/Grumpkin curves.

\item We introduce a novel architecture \textbf{FiZK}, for verifiable federated learning by leveraging the power of zero-knowledge proofs along with Byzantine-robust aggregation.

\item To achieve this, we introduce a \textbf{ZK-Firewall} to prove the correctness of the gradient information and also ensure that the norm of the gradient $(\|\nabla w_i\|_2 \leq B)$ is bounded before aggregation.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% ============================================================
% Most work on robust Federated Learning has focused either on statistical defenses or on cryptographic privacy; verifiable integrity at the level of individual client contributions is comparatively unexplored. Byzantine-robust aggregation rules like Krum \cite{blanchard2017machine}, coordinate-wise median \cite{yin2018byzantine}, and FLTrust \cite{cao2021fltrust} provide useful resilience or root-dataset-based heuristic, but they cannot supply pre-revelation gradient norm bounding or produce compact audit proofs. Blockchain-based FL \cite{kim2020blockchained} does offer global verifiability, yet the associated latency overhead is often impractical. Existing zero-knowledge proposals for FL have largely targeted server-side aggregation correctness rather than per-client gradient properties, and the broader zkML community has so far concentrated on verifiable inference.

% FiZK fills the gap between these lines of work. We use the ProtoGalaxy IVC scheme \cite{kothapalli2023protogalaxy}, accessed through the Sonobe library \cite{sonobe2024}, to build a lightweight two-constraint arithmetic circuit that checks gradient sum correctness and norm bounding. The result complements secure aggregation protocols like \cite{bonawitz2017practical}, which protect privacy through secret sharing but do not, on their own, provide the integrity guarantees or proof-compression efficiency that our architecture delivers.
Most existing work on robust Federated Learning follows a statistical approach or a cryptographic approach, while verifiable integrity of client contributions remains largely unexplored. Byzantine-robust aggregation mechanisms such as Krum \cite{blanchard2017machine}, coordinate-wise median \cite{yin2018byzantine}, and FLTrust \cite{cao2021fltrust} are proposed to provide robustness against malicious client updates or rely on a root dataset held by a server, but they do not provide pre-aggregation gradient norm constraints or verifiable proofs.

Another direction in this area is system-level transparency using a blockchain-based federated learning approach \cite{kim2020blockchained}, where global verifiability is provided, although this comes with a high latency and scalability cost. Concurrently, zero-knowledge techniques are starting to emerge in ML systems, although most recent work in zkML focuses on verifiable inference, not training. Zero-knowledge techniques proposed in existing work on federated learning verify the correctness of a server-side aggregation mechanism, not client gradients.

\subsection{Threat Model}
\label{sec:threat}

We consider a strong adaptive adversary that attacks concurrently at three levels. First, at the client level, up to $\alpha = 30\%$ of the clients can behave as Byzantine adversaries and may submit any gradients or strategically manipulate gradients to the aggregator, including label flipping, backdoors injection, model poisoning, or structured noise. Second, at the aggregator level, the aggregator can manipulate or tamper with the gradients or client identities. Finally, at the network level, an adversary can intercept or manipulate gradient messages.

We make the following assumptions:
(i) the local hardware of the clients is trusted;
(ii) SHA-256 and the ZK argument system used are computationally sound;
(iii) Messages can be delivered within a bounded time;
(iv) Every client has a unique registered key pair.

% ============================================================
\subsection{System Overview}
\label{sec:arch}
% ============================================================

FiZK has a two-level hierarchy for the participants, as depicted in Figure~\ref{fig:arch}. In the first level, the clients are divided into groups called \textbf{galaxies}. Each galaxy has a corresponding \textbf{Galaxy Aggregator} responsible for the collection of local commitments, zero knowledge verification, Byzantine aggregation, and proof compression. In the second level, the \textbf{Global Server} is responsible for collecting the verified results from each galaxy, conducting the cross-galaxy anomaly test, and the application of the update to the model.

In more technical terms, let $n$ denote the total number of clients. Let $G$ denote the number of galaxies. Then, the clients are divided into $G$ disjoint sets $\mathcal{C}_1, \mathcal{C}_2, \dots, \mathcal{C}_G$. Thus, $\bigcup_{g=1}^{G} \mathcal{C}_g = \mathcal{C}$. For each galaxy $g$, the corresponding \textbf{Galaxy Aggregator} computes the aggregated weights $\bar{w}_g$. It also computes the Merkle root $R_g$ for all client submissions. Furthermore, the \textbf{Galaxy Aggregator} computes the compact proof $\pi_g^{\mathrm{fold}}$. This proof is for the validity of all client computations without the need for the global server to reverify the proofs.

\subsection{Four-Phase Training Protocol}
\label{sec:protocol}
% ============================================================

Each training round goes through four phases shown in Figure~\ref{fig:arch}. The first two phases implement a \emph{commit-then-reveal} protocol: clients lock themselves to a gradient before exposing it, so any attempt at late-stage manipulation is cryptographically detectable.

\paragraph{Phase 1  Commitment and ZKP Generation}
Each client trains its model and calculates the gradients $\nabla w_i^{(r)}$. 
Before sending any information to the Galaxy Aggregator, each client generates two items (the Edge Clients block in Figure~1).

These items include the binding commitment
\[
h_i = H(\nabla w_i^{(r)} \,\|\, r \,\|\, \text{nonce}),
\]
where the gradient is hidden but locked to a specific value.

The zero-knowledge proof $\pi_i^{(\mathrm{zk})}$ is used to show that the gradients have been correctly summed. Moreover, the norm of the gradients is verified.

The bound is defined as
\[
B = \mathrm{median}\!\left(\left\{\|\nabla w_i^{(1)}\|_2\right\}_{i=1}^{n}\right)
\pm k \cdot
\mathrm{MAD}\!\left(\left\{\|\nabla w_i^{(1)}\|_2\right\}_{i=1}^{n}\right).
\]

This prevents any client from increasing the bound of its gradients.

The client sends $h_i$ and $\pi_i^{(\mathrm{zk})}$ to the Galaxy Aggregator. 
The aggregator forms a galaxy Merkle tree from all the hash values. Finally, the immutable root $R_g^{(r)}$ is published.

\paragraph{Phase 2  Gradient Revelation and ZK-Firewall}
After the locking of the gradients in the first phase, the gradients are revealed. A two-stage cryptographic gate is imposed by the \textit{Module: Layer~1 Crypto Integrity} (Figure~\ref{fig:arch}).

The \textbf{Merkle Lock} checks whether the hash of the revealed gradients is equal to the published root $R_g^{(r)}$. Should the hash of the revealed gradients fail to match the published root due to tampering or the involvement of a man-in-the-middle attacker, the gradients will be rejected.

In \textbf{ZK Firewall} the correctness of the gradients is checked. If the norm of the gradient is out of the permissible range B, then it is rejected.
% Together, these two checks constitute a hard cryptographic safety envelope around every submitted update.


\textbf{Claim 3.1 (Binding Commitment):}Suppose SHA-256 is still collision-free. Then, once the value of $R_g$ is revealed, it is impossible for an adversary to provide a gradient $w'$  with a different gradient from $\nabla w' \neq \nabla w_i$, such that the hash $h_i$ is the same.

\paragraph{Phase 3  Multi-Layer Defense and Proof Folding}
Gradient data is received and goes through the Filters \& Defense and Aggregation blocks as shown in Figure \ref{fig:arch}.

On the defense side, a statistical test is implemented in Layer 2. It tests the gradients against four different tests: norm, cosine direction, coordinate distribution, and KL divergence from the group. If gradients fail fewer than two of these four tests, they are rejected.

In Layer 3, the gradients are filtered using the multi-krum method to select gradients that are closest to each other and discard the gradients that are farther away from the group before computing the galaxy aggregate, represented as $\bar{w}_g$. In Layer 4, the client reputation score $\mathcal{R}_i$ is exponentially updated as follows:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{R}_i(t{+}1) = (1-\lambda)\,\mathcal{R}_i(t) + \lambda\, B_i(t), \quad \lambda = 0.1,
\end{equation}
where $\lambda$ is the learning rate and is fixed at 0.1, and $B_i(t)$ is the cumulative sum of pass/fail indicators.

On the proof side, the aggregation block uses ProtoGalaxy IVC Folding. It folds the proof as follows:
~\cite{kothapalli2023protogalaxy}

\begin{equation}
\pi_g^{\mathsf{fold}} = \mathcal{F}_{\mathsf{IVC}}\!\left(\dots\mathcal{F}_{\mathsf{IVC}}(\pi_1^{zk}, \pi_2^{zk})\dots, \pi_m^{zk}\right)
\end{equation}

As the size of the proof is independent of the value of $m$, the verification time at the Global Server is constant time complexity $O(1)$. Then the Galaxy Aggregator sends the triplet $(\bar{w}_g,\, \pi_g^{\mathsf{fold}},\, R_g)$  to the Global Server.

\paragraph{Phase 4  Global Verification and Model Update}

This Global Server (Figure~\ref{fig:arch})  processes all galaxies through a three-stage pipeline. First, the Merkle Verifier collects all the galaxy roots to create the global tree and verifies each  $R_g$ against the global root $R$. Then, the Layer 5 Galaxy Defense checks each galaxy’s aggregate update as a single entity to cross-check for anomalous galaxies by assessing norm deviations and similarities between galaxies. Finally, the Global Defense Pipeline aggregates the good updates to compute the new global weights $w^{(r+1)}$ for all participants.

\subsection{ZK-Firewall and IVC Folding}
\label{sec:zkp}
% ============================================================

The architecture's trustworthiness rests on two cryptographic mechanisms: the per-client ZK circuit and the galaxy-level IVC folding scheme.

\subsubsection{Client Zero-Knowledge Proof}

Each client proves, without revealing its gradient, that it belongs to the relation:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{R}_{\mathrm{zk}} = \left\{
(\nabla w_i, x_{\mathrm{pub}}) :
\begin{aligned}
&\textstyle\sum_j (\nabla w_i)_j = S_i \text{ (sum correctness)} \\
&\|\nabla w_i\|_2 \leq B
\end{aligned}
\right\}
\label{eq:zk_relation}
\end{equation}
where $B$ is set server-side before the round begins. Proofs are generated on BN254/Grumpkin curves via the Sonobe library~\cite{sonobe2024}.


\subsubsection{Galaxy-Level IVC Folding}

After the defense pipeline selects $m$ clients, the aggregator folds their proofs sequentially starting from $\mathrm{acc}_1 = \pi_1^{zk}$:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{acc}_{k} = \mathcal{F}_{\mathsf{IVC}}(\mathrm{acc}_{k-1},\, \pi_k^{zk}), \quad k = 2, \dots, m.
\label{eq:ivc_fold}
\end{equation}
The final accumulator $\pi_g^{\mathsf{fold}} = \mathrm{acc}_m$ is constant size independent of $m$. Galaxy IVC folding reduces $m$ per-client proof verifications to $O(1)$. Combined with the two-level Merkle tree, total server-side verification cost per round is $O(G \log n)$, where $G$ is the number of galaxies.

\subsubsection{Implementation}

The implementation is done using \textbf{Python}, with \textbf{PyTorch} used as the tool for local
model training. For the construction of the Merkle tree,
the SHA-256 hashing algorithm is used with the
Python standard library. For the zero-knowledge proof
construction and verification process,
the Rust language bridge is used with the
\textbf{Sonobe IVC library}~\cite{sonobe2024}, with the
ProtoGalaxy folding algorithm and
\textbf{BN254/Grumpkin} cycle curves. This Rust
language bridge is implemented using the
\textbf{Maturin} framework. For Byzantine robust aggregation,
the Multi-Krum method,
coordinate-wise median,
trimmed mean are used.
Among the techniques used for
statistical anomaly detection are
norm analysis,
cosine similarity,
coordinate-wise MAD,
KL-divergence.
For client reputation,
the EWMA (Exponentially Weighted Moving Average)
scheme is used.

\subsubsection{Experimental Setup Detail}
For the experiment setup, we use a focused 10 client proof of concept. While future work includes non-IID vision data sets of larger scale, this scale is adequate to prove O(1) verification compression of IVC folding.

\emph{Dataset}: MNIST\cite{lecun1998mnist} (60K train, 10K test) with IID partitioning.

\emph{Model}: Linear softmax classifier.

\emph{Hyperparameters}: Number of local training rounds $= 10$; local epochs $= 1$; batch size $= 64$; SGD optimizer with learning rate $0.01$; Byzantine proportion $\alpha$ in $\{0.20, 0.30, 0.40, 0.50\}$; ZK norm bound $B$ calculated from $\mathrm{median} \pm k \cdot \mathrm{MAD}$ of historical norms; trimmed mean aggregation with trim rate $0.1$.

\emph{Scale}: 10 clients in 2 galaxies (client distribution follows round-robin assignment rule $\text{client\_id} \bmod G$). Note that this is a small-scale experiment; larger-scale experiment designs are also in our roadmap (Section~\ref{sec:future}).

\emph{Baselines}: Vanilla FedAvg (no defense), Multi-Krum (standalone),
FiZK-Lite (Merkle~+~Multi-Krum, no ZKP), and
FiZK-Full (Merkle~+~ZKP~+~Multi-Krum~+~IVC folding).

\emph{Trials}: 3 independent trials per IID configuration (seeds 42, 43, 44); results reported as mean$\,\pm\,$std across trials. % [UPDATED 2026-03-10: was single trial seed=42]

\subsubsection{Attack Scenarios}

Six different types of attacks are considered:
(1) adaptive attack (The adversary is aware of the previous aggregations and adjusts the magnitude and direction of the gradients to evade detection.);
(2) label flipping ($0 \leftrightarrow 9$);
(3) Gaussian noise ($\nabla w_i +
\mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2 I)$);
(4) targeted label flip (only source class);
(5) model poisoning (gradient is scaled to make 
$\|\nabla w_i\|_2 \gg B$);
(6)~backdoor (pixel-pattern trigger causing targeted
misclassification).

\section{Limitations and Future Work}
\label{sec:future}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Limitations}
On the compute end, ZK proof generation in the client process happens in $O(d)$ with an overhead of 5.9× . This is mainly because the CPU-intensive proof process itself takes around $9.7 s$ to complete a proof and another $9.7 s$ to verify it per round. This can be a challenge for large models on resource-constrained clients. In the case of the Multi-Krum approach, the per-galaxy cost is $O(n^2 d)$, limiting the size of a single galaxy as well. As for the assignment policy, we currently use a simple round-robin approach. Finding an optimal assignment policy is an open question at the moment. Lastly, using Merkle trees alone does not guarantee gradient privacy; we need secure aggregation to keep the gradients confidential as well. Lastly, please note that the results we present here are at a proof-of-concept level with 10 clients, 2 galaxies, and MNIST as the only data set.
\subsection{Future Work}

Throughout our experiments we saw five potential direction worth pursuing.

First, the use of GPU acceleration for proof generation via libraries like Icicle or cuZK would significantly speed up proof times, as the current proof times are CPU-bound.

Second, the addition of dataset commitment and proof-of-training would improve the trust model by extending the ZK circuits for clients to prove training occurred on the committed data.

Third, the addition of privacy via the combination of ZK-Firewall with homomorphic encryption or differential privacy would add a new layer of confidentiality, along with the existing norm-bound guarantee.

Fourth, extensive evaluation with more clients (100+), aggregators, non-IID data splits, and more complex datasets like CIFAR-10 or medical image datasets is required for the practical applicability of the system.

Finally, the addition of global ProtoGalaxy folding, where the proofs for aggregating galaxy-level proofs are combined into a single proof, would allow the server’s verification time to be reduced to constant time, $O(1)$.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conc}
% ============================================================

% [UPDATED 2026-03-10: updated conclusion numbers from real 3-trial experiments]
We proposed FiZK, a hierarchical federated learning framework that combines zero-knowledge gradient validation, Merkle tree commitments, Byzantine aggregation, and IVC-based proof folding. At the heart of the framework is the ZK-Firewall, which enforces the gradient norm constraint ($\|\nabla w_i\|_2 \leq B$) and checks gradient sum correctness before aggregation. Galaxy-scale ProtoGalaxy proof folding compresses per-client proofs into a single constant-size accumulator $\pi_g^{\mathsf{fold}}$ per galaxy. Our experiments on MNIST with 10 clients and 3 independent trials demonstrate that FiZK-Full achieves \textbf{92.0\%} accuracy even at 50\% Byzantine majority under model poisoning, while Vanilla FedAvg degrades to 10.5\% and Multi-Krum collapses to 11.0\% --- an improvement of \textbf{+81pp}. FiZK-Full detects Byzantine clients with TPR~=~1.00, FPR~=~0.00 for model poisoning and F1~=~0.97--0.99 for backdoor attacks, with zero false positives across all 200 experiments. The cost is a ${\sim}9.5{\times}$ per-round overhead (37\,s vs 3.9\,s) from CPU-bound ZK proof operations, which can be addressed with GPU acceleration in future work.

\section{Full Round Protocol}
\label{app:algo}
% ============================================================

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{FiZK: One Training Round $r$}
\label{alg:round}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE global model $w^{(r)}$, norm bound $B$, Byzantine bound $f$
\ENSURE updated model $w^{(r+1)}$, galaxy proofs $\{\pi_g^{\mathsf{fold}}\}$

\STATE \textbf{// Phase 1: Client Commitment \& ZKP Generation}
\FOR{each client $i$}
  \STATE $\nabla w_i \leftarrow \mathsf{LocalTrain}(w^{(r)}, D_i)$
  \STATE $h_i \leftarrow H(\nabla w_i \;\|\; r \;\|\; \mathrm{nonce})$
  \STATE $\pi_i^{zk} \leftarrow \mathsf{ZKProve}(\nabla w_i, B)$
        \quad [proves sum correctness + $\|\nabla w_i\|_2 \leq B$]
  \STATE send $(h_i,\; \pi_i^{zk})$ to Galaxy Aggregator
\ENDFOR

\STATE \textbf{// Phase 2: Galaxy Verification \& ZK-Firewall}
\STATE $R_g \leftarrow \mathsf{MerkleRoot}(\{h_i\})$; publish $R_g$
\STATE $\mathcal{V} \leftarrow \emptyset$
\FOR{each client $i$}
  \STATE request $\nabla w_i$ from client $i$
  \IF{$\mathsf{MerkleVerify}(h_i, R_g) = \top$
       \AND $\mathsf{ZKVerify}(\pi_i^{zk}, B) = \top$}
    \STATE $\mathcal{V} \leftarrow \mathcal{V} \cup \{i\}$
  \ELSE
    \STATE \textbf{prune} client $i$; penalize reputation
  \ENDIF
\ENDFOR

\STATE \textbf{// Phase 3: Multi-Krum Selection \& IVC Folding}
\STATE $\mathcal{S},\;\bar{w}_g \leftarrow \mathsf{MultiKrum}(
  \{\nabla w_i\}_{i \in \mathcal{V}},\; f)$
\STATE $\pi_g^{\mathsf{fold}} \leftarrow \mathsf{IVC.Fold}(
  \{\pi_i^{zk}\}_{i \in \mathcal{S}})$
\STATE send $(\bar{w}_g,\; \pi_g^{\mathsf{fold}},\; R_g)$ to Global Server

\STATE \textbf{// Phase 4: Global Merkle Verification \& Update}
\STATE $R \leftarrow \mathsf{GlobalMerkleRoot}(\{R_g\}_{g=1}^G)$
\FOR{each galaxy $g$}
  \IF{$\mathsf{VerifyGalaxyRoot}(R_g, R) \neq \top$}
    \STATE reject galaxy $g$
  \ENDIF
\ENDFOR
\STATE $w^{(r+1)} \leftarrow w^{(r)} + \eta\,\sum_g \bar{w}_g / G$
\STATE broadcast $w^{(r+1)}$; log $R$
\RETURN $w^{(r+1)}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}
